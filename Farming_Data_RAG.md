### Seed Treatment

Treat the seeds with Trichoderma viride 4 g with rice kanji coating or fungicide treatment like Carbendazim 2 g per kg of seeds 24 hours before sowing. Sow the seeds in lines at 10 cm apart in raised nursery beds and cover with sand.
Raised bed Nursery

Soil should be of fine tilth and free from weeds.
Raised nursery beds should be prepared with height of 10-15 cm width of 1 m and of convenient length
Drench with 4% formaldehyde or 0.3% copper oxychloride to kill the pathogenic spores in the soil.
Solarization of nursery bed with white polythene sheet also check the nematode infection and weed growth
Two parts of fine red earth, one part of sand and one part of FYM can be incorporated to each bed to improve aeration and fertility of the soil
Lines are made on the nursery beds at 10-15 cm distance and line sowing is practiced
After sowing the seeds are covered with fine sand or powdered FYM
Nursery beds are covered with paddy straw and irrigation is given with rose can

Pro tray nursery

To cover 1 ha, prepare the nursery area for 3 cents with slanting slope of 2 % .
The nursery area should be covered with 50 % shade net and sides using 40/50 mesh insect proof nylon net.
Form raised beds of 1 m width and convenient length and place HDPV pipes at 2m interval for protection with polythene sheets during rainy months.

After six days, place the protrays with germinated seeds individually on the raised beds inside the shade net
Water with rosecan everyday and drench with NPK 19:19:19 @ 0.3% (3 g/l) at 18 days after sowing

### Protected Cultivation

Production Practices for Cultivation of Tomato Under Shade Net

During summer, the hybrid tomato can be grown in a shade level of 35 per cent under paired row planting system (80 x 40 x 60 cm - between pairs, rows and plants) with a basal application of 50 kg each of N and K and 250 kg of P / ha and fertigation of 200 kg each of N and K through straight fertilizers.

Protected Cultivation of Tomato in Polyhouse

During rainy season, the indeterminate tomato hybrid has to be grown in the medium consisting of FYM: composted coir pith: sand (2:1:1) with irrigation regime of 20kPa and basal application of 50kg each of NPK/ha as straight fertilizers and 250 kg each of NPK as water soluble and straight fertilizers through fertigation along with black polyethylene mulch (50 microns).

Nematodes of Tomato:
Root-knot nematode, Meloidogyne incognita

Root knot nematode, Meloidogyne incognita is a obligate, sedentary endoparasites. M. incognita is one of the major constraints in the production of tomato in tropical and subtropical regions. It causes 11 – 35% yield loss in tomato. Root knot remains a serious issue in both nursery and field conditions.

Mode of spread

Seedlings or planting materials, irrigation water and farm implements.

Symptoms

Above ground symptoms

Infested seedlings may not survive after transplanting.
Stunted growth of the plants.
Disease appearance in patches under field conditions.
Leaves show chlorosis and yellowing. Growth may be retarded. Plants show “Day wilting” symptom. Below ground symptoms

Primary galls are small and induced by swelling which coalesce to form larger secondary gall s/ knots on the root.
Root system is reduced to a severely galled and a completely disorganized vascular system.
Sometimes the galled tissues are invaded by secondary pathogens and results in root wilting.
                  

Management

Raised of seedlings in nematode free soil and use of nematode free planting materials.
Nursery application of Glomus fasciculatum@ 100g/m2.
Soil solarization in nursery beds with white or transparent sheet LLDPE 25 gauge thickness during summer.
Removal and destruction of nematode affected roots of previous crop.
Summer ploughing of field.
Crop rotation with non-host crops like mustard, sesame, maize, wheat and millets.
Intercroping with marigold @ 4:1 ratio.
Soil application of talc based formulation of Purpureocillium lilacinum @ 2.5 kg/ ha along with 250kg of FYM.
Soil application of carbofuran 3G @ 1 kg a.i./ha (33 kg/ha).

Leaf blight can be controlled by spraying Mancozeb @ 2.5 g/ litre.

Blanching

Blanching refers to covering of curds. A perfect curd of flower is pure white. It is necessary to exclude sunlight to obtain this. The common practice is to bring the outer leaves up over the curd and tie them with a twine or rubber band. By using a different coloured twine each day. It is easy at the time of harvest to select those tied earlier.

Physiological disorders

Browning or brown rot

This is caused by Boron deficiency. It appears as water soaked areas and later changes into rusty brown. Spray one kg of Borax in 500 lit of water 30 days after planting.

Whip tail

This results from the deficiency of Molybdenum. It is more pronounced in acidic soil. The leaf blades do not develop properly. In severe cases only the midrib develops and it can be corrected by spraying 100 g of Sodium molybdate in 500 lit of water 30 days after planting.

Buttoning

The term buttoning is applied to the development of small curds or buttons. The plants do not develop normally and leaves remain small and do not cover the developing curds. Deficiency of Nitrogen and planting the early varieties late may cause these symptoms. Avoid transplanting aged seedlings.

Blindness

Blind-cauliflower plants are those without terminal buds. The leaves are large, thick, leathery and dark green. It is due to the prevalence of low temperature when the plants are young or due to damage to the terminal bud during handling the plants or due to injury by pests.

Introduction
Banana is the fruit of a plant of the genus Musa (family Musaceae), which is cultivated primarily for food and secondarily for the production of fibre used in the textile industry are also cultivated for ornamental purposes. Almost all the modern edible parthenocarpic bananas come from the two wild species – Musa acuminataMusa balbisiana. The scientific names of bananas are Musa acuminata, Musa balbisiana or hybrids of Musa acuminata and balbisiana, depending on their genomic constitution.Bananas are vigorously growing, monocotyledonous herbaceous plants.The banana is not a tree but a high herb that can attain up to 15 meters of height. The cultivars vary greatly in plant an d fruit size, plant morphology, fruit quality and disease and insect resistance. Most bananas have a sweet flavor when ripe; exceptions to this are cooking bananas and plantains. Plantains are hybrid bananas in which the male flowering axis is either degenerated, lacking, or possess relicts of male flowers. Plantains are always cooked before consumption and are higher in starch than bananas. The two groups of plantains, French and Horn, produce fewer fruit per plant than sweet bananas. The groups differ in whether the male parts of the inflorescence are present or absent.

Papain has several industrial uses, the important one being in brewing industries. It is used as “meat tenderiser” and in textile and leather “sanforization” processes and drugs.

The method of extraction of papain from papaya fruits is simple. The latex should be tapped from immature papaya fruits. Select 75 to 90 days old fruits. On the selected fruit, give incisions (cut) with a razor blade or stainless steel knife. The cuts should be given from stalk to tip of the fruit. The depth of the cut should not be more than 0.3 cm. Four such cuts are given spaced equally on the fruit surface. Tap the latex early in the morning and complete the tapping before 10.00 a.m. Repeat the tapping four times on the same fruit at an interval of three days. The cut should be given on the fruit surface in places not covered by previous cuts. The latex collected from all the trees in a day should be pooled, shade dried in an aluminium pan or tray and passed through a 50 mesh sieve to remove all foreign matter. In large plantations, vacuum driers can be adopted with advantage. Papain produced by artificial heating will have better colour and high quality. Add potassium meta-bi-sulphite (KMS) at 0.5 % for better colour and keeping quality.

The latex should be dried very rapidly at temperatures of 50° to 55° C. Stop drying when the dried product comes off as flakes having a porous texture. Powder the dried papain by means of wooden mallets or in electrically operated granulators and sieve the powder through 10 mesh sieve. Pack the powder in polythene bags in convenient quantities and seal them. Put the sealed bags in a tin container and seal it after evacuating air. Exposure to air deteriorates the quality of papain and vacuum sealing is therefore necessary. For large scale manufacture of papain, vacuum sealing machine and a granulator will be useful.

The green papaya fruits after extraction of papain can be used for pectin manufacture and “tutty – fruity” or they can be allowed to ripen and made into other products. The Co.2 and Co.5 varieties of papaya released by Tamil Nadu Agricultural University, Coimbatore are ideal for papain production. Tamil Nadu Agricultural University, Coimbatore, supplies pure seeds of these varieties.

The watermelon (Citrullus lanatus) is a species of flowering plant in the family Cucurbitaceae, that has a large, edible fruit. It is a scrambling and trailing vine-like plant, and is widely cultivated worldwide, with more than 1,000 varieties.

Watermelons are grown in favorable climates from tropical to temperate regions worldwide for its large edible fruit, which is a berry with a hard rind and no internal divisions, botanically called a pepo. The sweet, juicy flesh is usually deep red to pink, with many seeds that are black when mature, white when immature. Seedless varieties also exist. The fruit can be eaten raw or pickled, and the rind is edible after cooking. It may also be consumed as a juice or an ingredient in mixed beverages.

Watermelon originated in Africa, where it was first cultivated. Kordofan melons from Sudan are the closest relatives and may be progenitors of modern, cultivated watermelons.[2] Wild watermelon seeds were found in Uan Muhuggiag, a prehistoric site in Libya that dates to approximately 3500 BC.[3] In 2022, a study was released that traced 6,000-year-old watermelon seeds found in the Libyan desert to the Egusi seeds of Nigeria, West Africa.[4] Watermelons were domesticated in Sudan and cultivated in Egypt by 2000 BC; an image of an apparent one on a tray has been found in an Egyptian tomb dating at least to 4000 years ago. Those were not of the sweet modern variety, but Hebrew texts from early in the Christian era place watermelon with other sweet table fruits, and dessert watermelons spread across the Mediterranean world during Roman antiquity.[5]

Considerable breeding effort has developed disease-resistant varieties. Many cultivars are available that produce mature fruit within 100 days of planting. As of 2023, China is the world's leading producer of watermelons with 64% of the total.

The watermelon is an annual that has a prostrate or climbing habit. Stems are up to 3 metres (10 feet) long and new growth has yellow or brown hairs. Leaves are 60 to 200 millimetres (2+1⁄4 to 7+3⁄4 inches) long and 40 to 150 mm (1+1⁄2 to 6 in) wide. These usually have three lobes that are lobed or doubly lobed. Young growth is densely woolly with yellowish-brown hairs which disappear as the plant ages. Like all but one species in the genus Citrullus, watermelon has branching tendrils. Plants have unisexual male or female flowers that are white or yellow and borne on 40-millimetre-long (1+1⁄2 in) hairy stalks. Each flower grows singly in the leaf axils, and the species' sexual system, with male and female flowers produced on each plant, is monoecious. The male flowers predominate at the beginning of the season; the female flowers, which develop later, have inferior ovaries. The styles are united into a single column.[citation needed]

The large fruit is a kind of modified berry called a pepo with a thick rind (exocarp) and fleshy center (mesocarp and endocarp).[6] Wild plants have fruits up to 20 cm (8 in) in diameter, while cultivated varieties may exceed 60 cm (24 in). The rind of the fruit is mid- to dark green and usually mottled or striped, and the flesh, containing numerous pips spread throughout the inside, can be red or pink (most commonly), orange, yellow, green or white.[7][8]

A bitter watermelon, C. amarus, has become naturalized in semiarid regions of several continents, and is designated as a "pest plant" in parts of Western Australia where they are called "pig melon".

The coconut (Cocos nucifera) is a member of the palm family (Arecaceae) and the only living species of the genus Cocos.[1] The term "coconut" (or the archaic "cocoanut")[2] can denote the whole coconut palm tree or the large hard fruit. Originally native to Central Indo-Pacific, they are ubiquitous in coastal tropical regions.

The coconut tree provides food, fuel, cosmetics, folk medicine and building materials. The inner flesh of the mature fruit forms a regular part of the diets of many people in the tropics and subtropics. Coconut endosperm contains a large quantity of a liquid, "coconut water". Mature coconuts can be processed for oil and coconut milk from the flesh, charcoal from the hard shell, and coir from the fibrous husk. Dried coconut flesh is called copra, and the oil and milk derived from it are commonly used in cooking and in soaps and cosmetics. Sweet coconut sap can be made into drinks or fermented into palm wine or coconut vinegar. The hard shells, fibrous husks and long pinnate leaves are used to make a products for furnishing and decoration.

The coconut has cultural and religious significance for Austronesian peoples, appearing in their mythologies, songs, and oral traditions. It has religious significance in South Asian cultures, where it is used in Hindu rituals including weddings and worship.

The species evolved in the central Indo-Pacific. It was domesticated by Austronesian peoples in Island Southeast Asia and spread during the Neolithic via their seaborne migrations as far east as the Pacific Islands, and as far west as Madagascar. The species played a critical role in their long sea voyages by providing a portable source of food and water, as well as building materials for outrigger boats. Coconuts were spread much later along the coasts of the Indian and Atlantic Oceans by South Asian, Arab, and from the 16th century by European sailors. Based on these introductions, the species can be divided into Pacific and Indo-Atlantic types. The Indo-Atlantic type was introduced to the Americas during the colonial era in the Columbian exchange, while Austronesian sailors appear to have introduced Pacific coconuts to Panama in pre-Columbian times.

Trees can grow up to 30 metres (100 feet) tall and can yield up to 75 fruits per year, though fewer than 30 is more typical. They are intolerant to cold and prefer copious precipitation and full sunlight. Many insect pests and diseases affect commercial production. In 2023, world production of coconuts was 65 million tonnes, with 73% of the total produced by Indonesia, India, and the Philippines.

Description
Cocos nucifera is a large palm, growing up to 30 metres (100 feet) tall, with pinnate leaves 4–6 m (13–20 ft) long, and pinnae 60–90 centimetres (2–3 ft) long; old leaves break away cleanly, leaving the trunk smooth.[3] On fertile soil, a tall coconut palm tree produces around 80 fruits per year; new varieties may be able to yield as many as 150 per year.[4] In India, average production is over 8,000 nuts per hectare per year.[5] Tall varieties produce their first fruit in 6 to 10 years, and live for 60 to 100 years; dwarf varieties become productive more quickly, but have a shorter lifespan.[6]

Inflorescence
The coconut is monoecious, meaning that both male and female flowers grow on the same tree, in its case in the same inflorescence.[7][8] It is possible that the species in addition occasionally has bisexual flowers.[9] The female flower is much larger than the male flower.[8] Mature trees grow continuously, producing leaves, flowers, and fruit all year round. It takes some 14 months for each flower primordium to develop into an inflorescence, botanically a spadix inside a sheathing spathe. A healthy tree can produce up to 15 inflorescences per year, staggered so that there is always a mature one with others in different stages of development.[10][11] It takes 11 months from the opening of the female flowers to the time of harvest.[12] Coconut palms are largely cross-pollinated, although most dwarf varieties are self-pollinating.[13]

Botanically, the coconut fruit is a drupe, not a true nut.[15] Like other fruits, it has three layers: the exocarp, mesocarp, and endocarp. The exocarp is the glossy outer skin, usually yellow-green to yellow-brown in color. The mesocarp is composed of a fiber, called coir, which has many traditional and commercial uses. The exocarp and the mesocarp make up the "husk" of the coconut, while the endocarp makes up the hard coconut "shell". The endocarp is around 4 millimetres (1⁄8 inch) thick and has three distinctive germination pores (micropyles) on the distal end. Two of the pores are plugged (the "eyes"), while one is functional.[16][17]

The interior of the endocarp is hollow and is lined with a thin brown seed coat, some 0.2 mm (1⁄64 in) thick. The endocarp is initially filled with a liquid endosperm (the coconut water). The liquid contains many free cell nuclei (it is multinucleate) dividing by mitosis, without cell boundaries. As development continues, cellular layers of endosperm deposit along the walls of the endocarp up to 11 mm (3⁄8 in) thick, starting at the far end. They eventually form the edible solid endosperm ("coconut meat") which hardens over time. The small cylindrical embryo is embedded in the solid endosperm directly below the functional pore. During germination, the embryo pushes out of the functional pore and forms a haustorium inside the central cavity. This absorbs the solid endosperm to nourish the seedling.[16][18][19]

The fruits have two distinctive forms. Wild niu kafa coconuts feature an elongated triangular fruit with a thicker husk and a smaller amount of endosperm. These make the fruits more buoyant, ideal for ocean dispersal. Domesticated niu vai Pacific coconuts are rounded in shape with a thinner husk, more endosperm, and more coconut water.[20][21][13]

A full-sized fruit weighs about 1.4 kilograms (3 pounds 1 ounce) depending on variety.[22][23] The 21st century Bido variety from Indonesia averages 3.158 kilograms (6 pounds 15.4 ounces) per fruit.[14]

Fruit with husk partly removed, showing hard thin shell of endocarp (left), coir fibre of exocarp (right)
Coconuts are exported without husks; de-husked nuts from Côte d'Ivoire average around 575 grams, while de-husked nuts from the Dominican Republic average nearly 700 grams.[24] Coconuts sold domestically in coconut-producing countries are typically not de-husked. Immature coconuts (6 to 8 months from flowering) are sold for coconut water and softer jelly-like coconut meat (known as "green coconuts", "young coconuts", or "water coconuts"), where the original coloration of the fruit is more pleasing.[22][23]

Whole mature coconuts (11 to 13 months from flowering) sold for export, however, typically have the husk removed to reduce weight and volume for transport. This results in the naked coconut "shell" with three pores, remnants of the three carpels of the flower, more familiar in countries where coconuts are not grown locally. De-husked coconuts are easier for consumers to open, but have a shorter postharvest storage life of around two to three weeks at temperatures of 12 to 15 °C (54 to 59 °F) or up to 2 months at 0 to 1.5 °C (32.0 to 34.7 °F). In comparison, mature coconuts with a husk can be stored for three to five months at room temperature.[22][23]

Roots
The palm tree has neither a taproot nor root hairs, but a fibrous root system.[7] This consists of many thin roots that grow outward from the plant near the surface. Only a few penetrate deep into the soil for stability. This is known as a fibrous or adventitious root system, and is a characteristic of grass species. 2,000–4,000 adventitious roots may grow, each about 1 cm (1⁄2 in) in diameter. Decayed roots are replaced regularly as the tree grows new ones.[25]

Taxonomy | Taxonomic history
The Swedish botanist and taxonomist Carl Linnaeus formally described the species Cocos nucifera in his book Species Plantarum in 1753.[26] The name is accepted by botanists.[1] In 1768, in his book The Gardeners Dictionary, the English botanist Philip Miller redescribed the plant as Palma cocos, a name treated as a synonym.[27][1] In 1891, the German botanist Otto Kuntze gave it the name Calappa nucifera in his Revisio Generum Plantarum, also treated as a synonym.[28][1]

The lemon (Citrus × limon) is a species of small evergreen tree in the Citrus genus of the flowering plant family Rutaceae. A true lemon is a hybrid of the citron and the bitter orange. Its origins are uncertain, but some evidence suggests lemons originated during the 1st millennium BC in what is now northeastern India. Some other citrus fruits are called lemon.

The yellow fruit of the lemon tree is used throughout the world, primarily for its juice. The pulp and rind are used in cooking and baking. The juice of the lemon is about 5–6% citric acid, giving it a sour taste. This makes it a key ingredient in drinks and foods such as lemonade and lemon meringue pie.

In 2024, world production was 23 million tonnes, led by India and Mexico with 31% of the total.

Description
The lemon tree produces a pointed oval yellow fruit. Botanically this is a hesperidium, a modified berry with a tough, leathery rind. The rind is divided into an outer colored layer or zest, which is aromatic with essential oils, and an inner layer of white spongy pith. Inside are multiple carpels arranged as radial segments. The seeds develop inside the carpels. The space inside each segment is a locule filled with juice vesicles.[2]

Lemons contain many phytochemicals, including polyphenols, terpenes, and tannins.[3] Their juice contains slightly more citric acid than lime juice (about 47 g/L), nearly twice as much as grapefruit juice, and about five times as much as orange juice.[4]

Lemons were most likely first grown in northwest India.[7] The origin of the word lemon may be Middle Eastern.[7] The word draws from the Old French limon, then Italian limone, from the Arabic ليمون laymūn or līmūn, and from the Persian لیمو līmūn, a generic term for citrus fruit, which is a cognate of Sanskrit (nimbū, 'lime').[8]

Lemons entered Europe near southern Italy no later than the second century AD, during the time of Ancient Rome.[7] They were later introduced to Persia and then to Iraq and Egypt around 700 AD.[7] The lemon was first recorded in literature in a 10th-century Arabic treatise on farming; it was used as an ornamental plant in early Islamic gardens.[7] It was distributed widely throughout the Arab world and the Mediterranean region in the Arab Agricultural Revolution between 1000 and 1150.[7] A section on lemon and lime tree cultivation in Andalusia, Spain, was included in Ibn al-'Awwam's 12th-century agricultural work, Kitāb al-Filāha ("Book on Agriculture").[9] The first substantial cultivation of lemons in Europe began in Genoa in the middle of the 15th century. It was introduced to the Americas in 1493, when Christopher Columbus brought lemon seeds to Hispaniola on his voyages. Spanish conquest throughout the New World helped spread lemon seeds, part of the Columbian exchange of plants between the Old and New Worlds. It was mainly used as an ornamental plant and for medicine.[7] In the 19th century, lemons were increasingly planted in Florida and California.[7] In 1747, the English physician James Lind's experiments on seamen suffering from scurvy involved adding lemon juice to their diets, though vitamin C was not yet known as an important dietary ingredient.[7][10]

Cultivation | Growing and pruning
Lemons need a minimum temperature of around 7 °C (45 °F), so they are not hardy year-round in temperate climates, but become hardier as they mature.[11] Citrus require minimal pruning by trimming overcrowded branches, with the tallest branch cut back to encourage bushy growth.[11] Throughout summer, pinching back tips of the most vigorous growth assures more abundant canopy development. As mature plants may produce unwanted, fast-growing shoots (called "water shoots"), these are removed from the main branches at the bottom or middle of the plant.[11] There is reputed merit in the tradition of urinating near a lemon tree.[12][13]

In cultivation in the UK, the cultivars "Meyer"[14] and "Variegata"[15] have gained the Royal Horticultural Society's Award of Garden Merit (confirmed 2017).[16]

Lemon juice and rind are used in a wide variety of foods and drinks, the juice for its sour taste, from its content of 5–6% citric acid.[28] The whole lemon is used to make marmalade,[29] lemon curd[30] and lemon liqueurs such as Limoncello.[31] Lemon slices and lemon rind are used as a garnish for food and drinks. It is used in marinades for fish, where its acid neutralizes amines in fish.[35] In meat, the acid partially hydrolyzes tough collagen fibers, tenderizing it.[36] In the United Kingdom, lemon juice is frequently added to pancakes eaten to celebrate Shrove Tuesday.[37] Lemon juice is used as a short-term preservative on certain foods that tend to oxidize and turn brown after being sliced (enzymatic browning), such as apples, bananas, and avocados: its acidity suppresses oxidation by polyphenol oxidase enzymes.[38]

Pouteria sapota, the mamey sapote, is a species of tree native to Central America and southern Mexico. It is now cultivated throughout Mexico, Central America, and the Caribbean, as well as in Florida and parts of South America. Its fruit is eaten raw in many Latin American countries, and is added to smoothies, milkshakes, ice cream, and other foods.

Description
Mamey sapote is a large evergreen tree that can reach a height of 15 to 45 m (49 to 148 ft) at maturity.[3] It is sometimes grown for its ornamental value in addition to its fruits.

The fruit, botanically a berry,[4] is about 10 to 25 cm (4 to 10 in) long and 8 to 12 cm (3 to 4.5 in) wide and has flesh ranging in color from pink to orange to red. The brown skin has a texture somewhat between sandpaper and the fuzz on a peach.[5] The fruit's texture is creamy and soft, and the flavor is a mix of sweet potato, pumpkin, honey, prune, peach, apricot, cantaloupe, cherry, and almond.[6][7][8] A mamey sapote is ripe when the flesh is vibrant salmon in color when a fleck of the skin is removed.[9] The flesh should give slightly, as with an overripe avocado. The leaves are pointed at both ends, 4 to 12 inches in length, and grow in clusters at the ends of branches.[10]

The mamey sapote is in the same family as, and thus closely botanically related to, other sapotes such as sapodilla (Manilkara zapota), abiu (P. caimito), and canistel (Lucuma campechiana). However, other fruits that go by the common name 'sapote' are less closely related, such as the black sapote (Diospyros nigra)[11] and white sapote (Casimiroa edulis).[12][13][14]

The native range probably extends from the southern Mexican states of Veracruz, Tabasco, and Chiapas through Nicaragua, Belize, and northern Honduras.[15] This is uncertain, however, because the tree was already widely cultivated in the tropical Americas prior to European colonization.[16] Today, it is cultivated in most Mexican states, many Caribbean islands, and tropical continental America from Florida to Brazil. Cultivation
It prefers smooth sandy, deep, clay and fertile soils, with pH of 5.5 to 6.5. The species does not tolerate low temperatures, prolonged periods of drought, or soils with bad drainage or where the water table is very high.[16]

It is mainly propagated by grafting, which ensures the new plant has the same characteristics as the parent, especially its fruit, as it does not grow true to seed. Grafting also produces fruiting trees considerably faster than growing by seed - grafted trees produce fruit in 3–5 years while trees grown from seed require 7 or more years of growth before fruiting.[19] Seeds lose viability within a month of harvest, so should be sown immediately.[16]

Pouteria sapota trees are quite productive fruiters. A typical mature tree may produce 200–500 fruits per year, while vigorous established trees may produce more than twice this amount.[17] The 2017 production average for Mexican growers was 12.4 tons/hectare.[20]

In Florida, the fruit is harvested from May to July with some cultivars producing year-round.[21][22]

### Pests and diseases
Pouteria sapota is not often troubled by significant insect damage. The Cuban May beetle (Phyllophaga bruneri) and the sugarcane rootstalk borer (Diaprepes abbreviatus), along with various species of scale and spider mites are known to attack the plant, but rarely is the infestation significant.[16]

Oviposition by fruit flies (Tephritidae) onto the fruit can cause damage to the pulp, although this appears to be deterred in P. sapota, perhaps related to its latex production and ability to expeditiously form cork on scars.[23] Among the most important species in this respect is Anastrepha serpentina, known as the sapote fly, specializing in Sapotaceae plants.[24]

# Cereals, Pulses, Spices and Medicinal Plants

## RICE
Rice is a semi-aquatic tropical crop that thrives in high temperatures and abundant moisture. It is typically grown in two seasons: the wet (Kharif) and dry (Rabi) seasons. For optimal growth, it requires clay or silt loam soils that can retain water, as most varieties are grown in flooded fields. It is a highly sensitive crop where biotic factors like variety and pests, and abiotic factors like sunlight intensity and water depth, directly dictate the final yield.

The cultivation process begins with meticulous land preparation using laser-leveling to ensure uniform water distribution. Farmers can choose between direct seeding (sowing seeds directly in the field) or the more common transplanting method. In transplanting, seedlings are first raised in a nursery for about 25–30 days before being moved to the main puddled field. Direct seeding requires more seeds (~60–80 kg/ha) but significantly less labor than manual transplanting.

During the growth phase, traditional lowland rice is kept under 2–5 cm of standing water until about a week before harvest. This constant flooding acts as a natural weed suppressant. However, modern techniques like "Alternate Wetting and Drying" (AWD) are now used to save water by allowing the field to dry slightly before re-flooding. The plant typically reaches 3–4 feet in height over a period of 120 to 150 days, depending on the variety.

Rice is susceptible to a variety of pests including stem borers, leafhoppers, and gall midges, as well as diseases like rice blast and sheath blight. High nitrogen application can often worsen pest outbreaks like aphids. Integrated Pest Management (IPM) involving traditional methods like duck-farming or modern biopesticides is recommended for amateurs to keep the crop healthy without excessive chemical use.

Harvesting begins when the moisture content of the grain drops to 20–25% and the panicles turn golden yellow. After reaping and threshing to separate the grain from the stalks, the "paddy" must be dried immediately to below 14% moisture to prevent mold. Traditional sun-drying on mats is common for beginners, while commercial farms use forced-air dryers before the milling process.
##
WHEAT
Wheat is primarily a temperate-zone crop but is widely grown in India as a Rabi (winter) crop. It requires a cool growing season followed by a bright, dry ripening period. Well-drained loamy soils are ideal. Winter wheat varieties require a process called "vernalization"—a period of cold temperatures (0° to 5°C)—to trigger the flowering stage. Without this cold snap, the plant may remain in a vegetative state and fail to produce grain.

Land preparation involves multiple stirrings of the soil to create a fine, firm seedbed which protects the seeds during winter dormancy. Sowing is usually done in rows using a seed drill at a depth of 4–5 cm. Nitrogen is the most critical nutrient for wheat; it must be applied at the time of sowing and during the "crown root initiation" stage (about 21 days after sowing) to ensure a strong start and high tillering.

Irrigation management is vital, especially at critical stages like flowering and grain filling. While wheat is more water-efficient than rice, drought stress during the heading stage can lead to shriveled grains. Amateurs should monitor soil moisture closely in the spring when growth accelerates. Proper drainage is equally important, as waterlogging can lead to root rot and nutrient leaching.

Common threats to wheat include fungal diseases like rust (brown, black, and yellow) and loose smut. Pests like aphids and termites can also damage the crop, especially in drier regions. Using resistant varieties and treated seeds is the most effective way for beginners to manage these risks. Regular weeding during the first 30–45 days is essential to prevent competition for nutrients.

The crop is ready for harvest when the straw becomes yellow and brittle, and the grains become hard (they should "click" when bitten). In India, harvesting usually occurs between March and May. Harvested bundles are dried in the sun for a few days before threshing. To prevent storage pests like weevils, the grain must be dried until the moisture content is below 12%.
##
MAIZE (CORN)
Maize is often called the "Queen of Cereals" because of its high genetic yield potential and adaptability to diverse climates. It can be grown in almost any soil type, provided there is good drainage and high fertility. Unlike many other crops, maize has no wild variety and depends entirely on human intervention for propagation. It requires a well-tilled soil with a fine tilth, usually achieved by multiple rounds of plowing and harrowing.

Sowing for the Kharif season typically starts with the onset of the monsoon in June, while Rabi maize is sown in October-November. Seeds should be treated with fungicides like Carbendazim to prevent soil-borne diseases. A seed rate of 5–6 kg per acre is standard, with seeds sown at a depth of 3–5 cm in shallow furrows. Proper spacing (usually 60 cm between rows) is crucial to allow enough sunlight for the lower leaves.

The growth process is rapid after the third week. Maize is a "heavy feeder," requiring significant amounts of organic manure (7–8 tons per acre) and NPK fertilizers. Irrigation should be frequent but controlled; while the crop needs moisture, water stagnation can kill the plants in their early stages. Critical stages for irrigation are the "tasseling" (male flower) and "silking" (female flower) stages.

Downy mildew and leaf blight are the most common diseases, often managed by spraying Mancozeb. Stem borers are a major pest; they tunnel into the stalks and can be controlled by applying granular insecticides in the leaf whorls. For amateurs, "stay-green" varieties are popular because the stalks remain green and can be used as fodder even after the ears are harvested.

Harvesting is done when the cob’s outer husk turns brown and the grains are hard and dry. For sweet corn, the harvest is much earlier, when the kernels are in the "milk" stage. After harvest, the cobs are de-husked and sun-dried until the moisture level is around 13–14% for safe shelling and storage.
##
GROUNDNUT (PEANUT)
Groundnut is a unique legume that matures its pods underground. It thrives in well-drained, loose sandy loam soils, which allow the "pegs" to easily penetrate the soil. It is primarily a rainfed Kharif crop in India, sown between mid-June and mid-July. Since groundnuts are sensitive to soil acidity, applying lime every 4–5 years is recommended to maintain a neutral pH and provide calcium, which is essential for pod filling.

The sowing process involves using 60–80 kg of seeds per hectare. A key amateur tip is to ensure "seed-to-soil contact" by firming the soil after sowing. Before planting, seeds should be treated with Thiram or Rhizobium culture to boost nitrogen fixation. The field should be plowed deeply once and then harrowed to ensure a fine, weed-free seedbed that doesn't crust over.

A fascinating part of the growth process is "pegging." After the flowers are fertilized above ground, they wither and a stalk-like structure (the peg) grows downward into the soil where the pod develops. During this time, soil must remain loose. Farmers should avoid hoeing or weeding once pegging starts to prevent damaging the developing pods. Irrigation is most critical during the flowering and pod-filling stages.

Common pests include aphids, which spread the Rosette virus, and white grubs that eat the roots. "Tikka" leaf spot is a frequent fungal disease that causes dark brown spots and premature leaf shedding. Maintaining a proper crop rotation with cereals like maize or wheat can help break the cycle of these pests and maintain soil health.

Harvesting takes place when the leaves start turning yellow and shedding. A simple test for maturity is to pull up a few plants and check the inside of the pods; a dark brown or blackish inner shell indicates ripeness. After uprooting, the plants are left to dry in the sun for 5–7 days. The pods are then separated and stored once the moisture drops below 10% to prevent the growth of toxic Aspergillus molds.
##
SUNFLOWER
Sunflower is an adaptable oilseed crop that can be grown year-round but performs best in fertile, well-drained soils. It is particularly known for its deep taproot system, which makes it more drought-tolerant than many other crops. However, for a successful start, it requires a firm seedbed and a soil temperature of at least 10°C (50°F) for uniform germination.

Sowing is typically done at a shallow depth of about 1 inch. While the crop is drought-tolerant, water stress during the "inflorescence emergence" (when the head begins to form) can severely reduce seed count. Amateurs should monitor root-zone moisture during this stage. It is also important not to over-fertilize with nitrogen in the early stages, as this can lead to excessive foliage growth at the expense of oil content and yield.

The plant goes through several distinct stages: germination, leaf development, stem elongation, flowering, and seed ripening. During the "heading" stage, the flower heads exhibit heliotropism (following the sun). Nutrients like phosphorus and boron are vital during flowering to ensure that the seeds are well-filled and not empty (chaffy).

Sunflowers are susceptible to downy mildew, especially if planted too deep in wet soil. Another major threat is the "Broomrape" parasite, which attaches to the roots and can cause total crop failure. Birds are also a significant "pest" during the ripening stage, often requiring manual scaring or netting in small-scale amateur farms to protect the seeds.

Harvesting is ready when the back of the flower head turns lemon yellow or brownish, and the moisture content of the seeds is around 10–12%. If the harvest is delayed, the seeds may shatter and fall to the ground. After cutting the heads, they are dried and threshed. The seeds should be stored in a cool, dry place to prevent the oil from turning rancid.
##
SOYBEAN
Soybean is a high-protein legume that is classified into two types: indeterminate (continues growing after flowering) and determinate (stops vertical growth after flowering). It is a "short-day" plant, meaning flowering is triggered when days become shorter than a specific threshold (usually 12.8 hours). It grows best in well-drained, fertile soils with a pH between 6.0 and 7.5.

Sowing depth is critical; seeds should be planted about 1–2 inches deep. If planted too deep, the hypocotyl may fail to pull the cotyledons to the surface. Since soybeans fix their own nitrogen through a symbiotic relationship with Rhizobium bacteria, ensuring these bacteria are present in the soil or applied as a seed treatment is the single most important step for an amateur farmer.

The growth stages are divided into Vegetative (V) and Reproductive (R). After emergence, the first 7–10 days of nutrition are provided by the cotyledons themselves. By the time the third node appears, root nodulation should be active. The plant requires consistent moisture during the "pod-fill" stage; drought at this time will result in smaller seeds and significantly lower protein content.

The main pests are pod borers and girdle beetles, which can be managed by timely scouting. Soybeans are also prone to the Yellow Mosaic Virus. A unique feature of soybeans is that they often drop their leaves entirely before the seeds are fully mature. This is a natural part of the "senescence" stage and not necessarily a sign of disease.

Harvesting is done when the pods are dry, brown, and the seeds "rattle" inside when shaken. The moisture content should be around 13–15% for mechanical harvesting to prevent seed cracking. Because soybean seed coats are fragile, they must be handled gently during threshing and storage to maintain high germination rates for the next season.
##
COTTON
Cotton is a warm-season woody perennial shrub grown commercially as an annual. It requires a long, frost-free period, plenty of sunshine, and moderate rainfall (50–100 cm). While it is somewhat salt and drought-tolerant, high-yielding commercial varieties often rely on heavy irrigation. It grows best in "Black Cotton Soil" (Regur), which has high water-holding capacity, but can also thrive in well-drained alluvial soils.

Preparation begins in the autumn after the previous harvest. Planting usually happens from February to June depending on the region. Since cotton is naturally a perennial, growing it as an annual is a key strategy for pest control. Amateurs should choose "Bt Cotton" varieties, which are genetically modified to produce a natural protein that kills bollworms, the crop's most destructive pest.

The growth of the fiber (lint) happens in three stages after flowering: elongation (cell grows long), thickening (cellulose is deposited), and maturation (fiber dries and twists). This process takes about 50–60 days per boll. During this time, the plant must be protected from "sucking pests" like jassids and whiteflies, which can be managed with neem-based sprays.

Irrigation is most critical during the flowering and boll-development stages. However, too much water or nitrogen late in the season can cause "vegetative luxuriance," where the plant keeps growing leaves but stops producing bolls. To prevent this, some farmers use "defoliants" to shed leaves and help the bolls open uniformly before harvest.

Harvesting is done by picking the white, fluffy lint from the open bolls. In India, this is mostly done manually in 3–4 "pickings" as the bolls mature at different times. The picked cotton (kapas) contains both fiber and seeds. It is then sent for "ginning," where the fiber is separated from the seeds. The fiber is pressed into bales, while the seeds are crushed for oil or used as cattle feed.
##
SUGARCANE
Sugarcane is a giant perennial grass grown primarily in tropical and subtropical regions. It is a "thirsty" and "hungry" crop, requiring high temperatures, bright sunlight, and around 150–250 cm of water annually. It is unique because it is not grown from seeds but from "setts"—sections of the stem containing one or more buds. It thrives in heavy, fertile soils with good drainage.

The planting process involves digging furrows and laying the setts horizontally before covering them with soil. A common practice for amateurs is "Ratooning," where after the first harvest, the roots are left in the ground to grow a second or third crop (the ratoon). This saves the cost of new setts and land preparation. Sugarcane can fix some of its own nitrogen through a relationship with bacteria living inside its stems.

Growth occurs in four stages: germination, tillering (producing multiple stalks), grand growth (rapid stalk elongation), and ripening. During the grand growth stage, which lasts 4–5 months, the plant needs heavy irrigation and fertilizer. The stalks can reach up to 5 meters in height. It is important to "tie" the stalks together (propping) to prevent them from falling over (lodging) during heavy winds.

Major pests include the cane beetle or "grub," which eats the roots, and the sugarcane borer. Diseases like "Red Rot" (which makes the inside of the stalk turn red and smell like alcohol) are very common and can destroy the whole crop. Using disease-free setts and avoiding waterlogging are the best preventive measures for beginners.

Harvesting is a critical timing task. It must be done before the plant flowers, as flowering consumes the stored sugar. Farmers check the "Brix" (sugar content) using a hand refractometer or by looking for signs like the leaves turning yellow and the stalks sounding metallic when tapped. The stalks are cut as close to the ground as possible, as the bottom part has the highest sugar concentration.
##
TURMERIC
Turmeric is a rhizomatous perennial plant native to the Indian subcontinent. It requires a warm, humid climate with temperatures between 20°C and 30°C and heavy rainfall. It is almost always grown as an annual for spice production. The crop prefers well-drained, loose, friable loamy or alluvial soils rich in organic matter. It cannot tolerate waterlogging, which quickly causes the rhizomes to rot.

Propagation is done using "mother rhizomes" or "finger rhizomes." Planting usually happens in May-June with the start of the monsoon. The rhizomes are planted in raised beds or ridges to ensure good drainage. Amateurs should be aware that turmeric is a long-duration crop, taking 7 to 9 months to reach maturity. It is often intercropped with maize or chilies to maximize land use.

The growth process starts slowly, with sprouts appearing about 2–4 weeks after planting. As the "false stems" (made of leaf sheaths) grow, the plant requires regular weeding and earthing-up (piling soil around the base) to encourage rhizome expansion. Frequent irrigation is necessary, especially during the rhizome development phase from September to November.

The most serious threat is "Rhizome Rot," caused by soil-borne fungi during periods of heavy rain and poor drainage. Pests like the "Shoot Borer" can also damage the stems. Using organic mulch (like dried leaves) immediately after planting is a classic "pro tip" for amateurs—it preserves moisture, suppresses weeds, and adds organic matter as it decomposes.

Harvesting begins when the leaves and stems turn dry and yellow. The rhizomes are carefully dug out without bruising them. Post-harvest processing is what gives turmeric its vibrant color: the rhizomes are boiled in water until they are soft, then dried in the sun for 10–15 days until they become hard and brittle. Finally, they are polished to remove the outer skin before being ground into powder.
##
RED GRAM (PIGEON PEA / TUR)
Red Gram is one of the most important pulse crops in India, valued for its high protein content. It is a hardy, drought-tolerant legume that can grow in various soil types, though it performs best in well-drained loams. A key feature for farmers is its deep taproot system, which breaks up hard soil and brings up nutrients from deeper layers, making it excellent for soil health.

Sowing is typically done in June-July. It is an "indeterminate" plant that can grow into a tall shrub (2–3 meters). Because it grows slowly in the first two months, it is almost always intercropped with faster-growing crops like sorghum or pearl millet. This "doubles" the farmer's output from the same piece of land while the Red Gram slowly establishes itself.

The growth process is unique because the plant enriches the soil through symbiotic nitrogen fixation in its root nodules. It requires very little nitrogen fertilizer but responds well to phosphorus. While it is drought-tolerant, providing one or two irrigations during the flowering and pod-development stages can significantly "boost" the yield for an amateur farmer.

The "Pod Borer" (Helicoverpa) is the most devastating pest, often attacking the flowers and young pods. Diseases like "Wilt" and "Sterility Mosaic" are also common. Amateurs should look for "Wilt-resistant" varieties. Proper spacing (about 60–90 cm between rows) is essential to allow for spraying and to reduce the humidity that encourages fungal growth.

Harvesting begins when about 75–80% of the pods turn brown and dry. The plants are cut at the base, tied into bundles, and left to dry for a few days. Threshing is then done by beating the plants with sticks or using a mechanical thresher. The seeds are dried until the moisture is around 10–12% before being stored or split into "dal."
##
BLACKGRAM (URD / MASH)
Blackgram is a short-duration pulse crop (60–90 days) that is widely grown in India across three seasons: Kharif, Rabi, and Summer. It is a warm-weather crop that requires a hot and humid climate. It grows well on a variety of soils, from sandy to heavy "black cotton" soils, but cannot tolerate salinity or alkalinity. It is a popular choice for "Rice Fallows," where it is sown immediately after the rice harvest using the residual moisture in the soil.

Sowing for the Kharif season happens in June-July, while Summer sowing is in February-March. Seeds should be treated with fungicides and Rhizobium culture. For an amateur, Blackgram is an "easy" crop because of its short lifecycle; it requires a relatively small amount of water and fertilizer compared to cereals. The seed rate is usually 12–15 kg per hectare for the Kharif season.

During growth, the plant forms a low, spreading bush. It is very effective at covering the soil, which helps prevent erosion and suppresses weeds. While it is mostly rainfed, if grown in the summer, it needs irrigation every 10–15 days. Heavy rains during the flowering stage are harmful as they can wash away the pollen and prevent pod setting.

The "Yellow Mosaic Virus" (YMV), spread by whiteflies, is the most common disease and can turn entire fields yellow and sterile. Choosing YMV-resistant varieties is the best strategy for beginners. Pests like aphids and pod borers can also occur. Since it is a legume, it fixes nitrogen, so only a small "starter" dose of nitrogen is needed at sowing.

Harvesting is done when the pods turn black and are completely dry. Unlike other pulses, Blackgram pods can "shatter" (burst open) if they become too dry in the sun, so it is best to harvest in the early morning when there is some dew. The plants are uprooted or cut, dried for a few days, and then threshed. The grains are cleaned and dried to 10% moisture for storage.

CORIANDER (Coriandrum sativum)
Coriander is a versatile annual herb where both the leaves (cilantro) and the dried seeds are used. It thrives in a cool, frost-free climate with optimal temperatures between 20°C and 25°C. For high seed yield, it requires a well-drained loamy soil with a pH range of 6.0 to 8.0. While it can be grown year-round for leaves, seed production is best suited for the Rabi (winter) season in India.

Sowing requires a seed rate of 10–12 kg/ha for grain and 20–25 kg/ha for leaf production. A unique amateur tip: the "seeds" are actually fruits containing two seeds each; they must be gently crushed (split) before sowing to ensure better germination. Seeds should be sown at a depth of 3 cm with a spacing of 30 x 10 cm. Germination typically takes 10–15 days.

The growth process is rapid, reaching the leaf-harvesting stage in just 35–40 days. For seed production, the crop matures in 90–110 days. It is a "thirsty" herb in its early stages, requiring irrigation every 7–10 days. Nitrogen application is critical for leaf growth; applying 20 kg N/ha after the first leaf cutting can significantly boost the second growth.

The most devastating disease is Powdery Mildew (Erysiphe polygoni), which appears as white flour-like patches on leaves. It can be managed by spraying 0.2% Wettable Sulphur. Pests like aphids (Hyadaphis coriandri) often attack the flowering umbels. For beginners, using neem-based sprays at a concentration of 3% is an effective organic control.

Harvesting for leaves is done by pulling the whole plant or cutting the base. For seeds, harvest when the fruits turn from green to brown/yellow and the moisture content is around 15%. Post-harvest, seeds must be dried in the shade to a moisture level of 9% to preserve the essential oil, Linalool, which gives coriander its distinct aroma.

FENUGREEK (Trigonella foenum-graecum)
Fenugreek (Methi) is a cool-season legume used as a leafy green and a spice. It is highly adaptable but prefers well-drained loamy soils with a pH of 6.0–7.0. It is relatively frost-tolerant but sensitive to heavy rains during flowering. The plant is a nitrogen-fixer, making it an excellent "rotation crop" to improve soil fertility for future vegetables.

The sowing process uses a seed rate of 20–25 kg/ha. Seeds should be treated with Rhizobium culture to enhance nitrogen fixation. Sowing is done in rows 20–25 cm apart with a plant-to-plant distance of 10 cm. Germination is very fast, often appearing within 3–5 days if the soil temperature is around 15°C–20°C.

During growth, the plant requires 4–5 irrigations throughout its 90–120 day lifecycle. Being a legume, it needs low nitrogen but high phosphorus (25–40 kg P2O5/ha). For amateur "Microgreen" farming, fenugreek can be harvested as early as 10 days, while the main leafy crop is ready for the first cut at 30 days.

The primary disease threat is Downy Mildew, which causes yellowing on the upper leaf surface. Pests include the lucerne caterpillar, which can be controlled by hand-picking in small gardens. Proper drainage is the best preventive measure, as fenugreek is highly susceptible to root rot in waterlogged conditions.

Harvesting for seeds occurs when the pods turn yellowish and the bottom leaves start falling. The pods are usually 7–10 cm long and contain 10–20 seeds each. To prevent "shattering" (seeds falling out), harvest in the early morning. Seeds are dried to a moisture content of 10% for storage.

GINGER (Zingiber officinale)
Ginger is a tropical perennial herb grown as an annual for its pungent rhizomes. It requires a warm and humid climate with heavy rainfall (150–300 cm) and an altitude of 600–1500 meters. The ideal soil is a sandy or clayey loam rich in humus, with a pH of 6.0–6.5. It is a shade-loving plant, making it perfect for intercropping under coconut or areca nut trees.

Propagation is done using "seed rhizomes" weighing 20–30 grams each, containing at least one or two "eyes" (buds). The seed rate is high, approximately 1500–1800 kg/ha. Planting happens in April-May after the first pre-monsoon showers. Rhizomes are planted in pits or trenches at a spacing of 25 x 25 cm and a depth of 5 cm.

The growth process takes 8–9 months. Ginger is a "heavy feeder"; it requires 100 kg N, 50 kg P, and 50 kg K per hectare. A "pro-tip" for amateurs: Mulching is mandatory. Apply 15 tonnes/ha of green leaves immediately after planting and again at 60 and 90 days to preserve moisture and prevent soil erosion.

The most serious disease is Soft Rot (Pythium spp.), a soil-borne fungus that turns rhizomes into a watery mass. To prevent this, treat seed rhizomes with 0.3% Mancozeb for 30 minutes before planting. Pests like the Shoot Borer can be identified by the yellowing of the central leaf (dead heart).

Harvesting depends on the end use: for "Green Ginger," harvest at 7 months; for "Dry Ginger," harvest at 8–9 months when leaves turn yellow and dry. Rhizomes are washed thoroughly and dried. For dry ginger, the outer skin is scrapped (peeled) and the rhizome is dried in the sun for 8–10 days until the moisture is reduced to 10%.

GARLIC (Allium sativum)
Garlic is a bulbous perennial grown as a winter crop in India. It requires a cool, moist period during growth and a warm, dry period for maturity. It thrives in well-drained loamy soils rich in organic matter. A pH of 6.0 to 7.0 is ideal. High nitrogen is required, but excessive amounts late in the season can cause "secondary growth" or splitting of the bulbs.

Planting is done using individual "cloves" (the segments of the bulb). Use 500–600 kg of cloves per hectare. Cloves are planted at a depth of 2–3 cm with the pointed end facing up. Spacing should be 15 x 10 cm. Before planting, cloves should be treated with a fungicide to prevent bulb rot.

The growth stages include the vegetative phase and the bulb-filling phase. Garlic requires frequent but light irrigation every 10–15 days. If the soil dries out completely during bulb formation, the bulbs will be small and "cloved" poorly. Stop irrigation 3 weeks before harvest to allow the outer skin to dry and harden.

Common problems include Thrips, which are tiny insects that suck sap and turn leaves silvery. These can be managed with 0.05% Monocrotophos. Diseases like Purple Blotch cause sunken spots on leaves. For amateurs, ensuring Crop Rotation (not planting garlic/onions in the same spot for 3 years) is the best defense.

Harvesting is ready when the tops turn yellow/brown and start to collapse (120–150 days). After pulling the bulbs, they must be "cured" (dried in the shade) for 7–10 days. This process hardens the outer skin and increases shelf life. Properly cured garlic can be stored for 6–8 months at room temperature.

ALOE VERA (Aloe barbadensis)
Aloe Vera is a hardy, succulent perennial known for its medicinal gel. It is a "low-maintenance" plant that thrives in high temperatures and low rainfall, making it ideal for arid regions. It prefers well-drained sandy soils but can grow in poor soils with a pH up to 8.5. It is highly sensitive to waterlogging, which causes rapid root rot.

Propagation is done using "Suckers" (baby plants that grow from the base of the parent). For a commercial setup, 37,000 to 55,000 suckers/ha are used. Sowing is usually done in the rainy season at a spacing of 60 x 45 cm. The suckers are planted at a depth of 10–15 cm, ensuring the heart of the plant is not covered with soil.

The growth process is slow but steady. Aloe Vera needs minimal fertilizer—around 10 tonnes/ha of farmyard manure is usually enough. Irrigation should be very sparse; once every 2 weeks in summer and not at all during the monsoon. Over-watering is the #1 cause of death in amateur Aloe farming.

The plant is generally pest-resistant, but it can suffer from Aloe Rust (black spots on leaves) in humid conditions. Mealybugs sometimes hide in the base of the leaves. For an amateur, simply wiping the leaves with a water-vinegar solution or neem oil is sufficient control.

Harvesting begins 10–12 months after planting. Only the bottom-most, fully mature leaves (at least 30 cm long) should be cut. A healthy plant can be harvested 3-4 times a year for up to 5 years. The leaves should be processed within 6 hours of cutting, as the gel oxidizes and loses its medicinal potency quickly.

ASHWAGANDHA (Withania somnifera)
Ashwagandha, also known as "Indian Ginseng," is a major medicinal shrub. It is a drought-tolerant crop that grows well in sub-tropical regions. It requires a dry season for root development and cannot tolerate heavy rains or waterlogging. The best soil is well-drained, light red or sandy loam with a pH of 7.5 to 8.0.

Sowing is usually done by "Broadcasting" (scattering seeds) at a rate of 10–12 kg/ha. If the soil is very fertile, the roots become "leafy" and poor in medicinal value; therefore, Ashwagandha is often grown on "marginal" or poor lands. Seeds should be sown just before the monsoon. Thinning is done 25–30 days later to maintain a plant population of 20,000–25,000 per hectare.

The growth process lasts 150–180 days. The plant reaches a height of 30–75 cm. The most important part is the root, which contains alkaloids like Withanine. One or two life-saving irrigations are needed if the monsoon fails. No heavy fertilizer is required, as chemical nitrogen can reduce the quality of the medicinal alkaloids.

The crop is relatively pest-free, but Seedling Blight and Leaf Spot can occur during humid spells. Spraying 0.3% Dithane M-45 is a common control. For an amateur, ensuring the soil has excellent drainage is the primary management task.

Harvesting starts in January-March when the leaves turn yellow and the berries turn bright red. The whole plant is uprooted. The roots are separated from the stem, washed, and dried in the shade. Roots are then graded by length: Grade A roots are 7 cm long and 1.5 cm thick, fetching the highest price.
